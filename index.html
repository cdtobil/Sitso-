Regulatory Framework & Legal Basis
8 Questions
01
LawFoundation

What is the primary legislation governing insurance regulation in Ghana, and what are its key objectives? Core

▾
Model Answer
The primary legislation is the Insurance Act, 2021 (Act 1061), which repealed and replaced the Insurance Act, 2006 (Act 724). Its key objectives include:
  • Regulating and supervising the insurance industry to ensure its soundness and stability
  • Protecting policyholders and insurance consumers
  • Promoting fair, transparent, and competitive market conduct
  • Facilitating the development of the insurance sector in Ghana
  • Aligning domestic regulation with international standards such as the IAIS Insurance Core Principles (ICPs)
Act 1061 also strengthened the NIC's enforcement powers, introduced risk-based supervision, and expanded requirements around market conduct and consumer protection — areas central to this role.
02
MandateNIC Powers

What enforcement powers does the National Insurance Commission (NIC) have under Act 1061? Core

▾
Model Answer
Under Act 1061, the NIC holds wide-ranging enforcement powers, including:
  • Licensing and revocation — granting, suspending, or revoking licences of insurers, brokers, agents, and loss adjusters
  • Administrative sanctions — issuing fines, penalties, and compliance directives
  • On-site inspections and investigations — conducting routine and special examinations of licensees' books, records, and operations
  • Remedial actions — directing an insurer to recapitalise, restructure, or submit a corrective action plan
  • Judicial referral — referring matters of criminal conduct (fraud, misrepresentation) to the Attorney-General or law enforcement
  • Appointment of an administrator or conservator for a failing insurer to protect policyholders
These powers apply across the full insurance value chain — from the insurer itself to intermediaries and ancillary service providers.
03
LicensingCategories

What classes of insurance business are regulated under Act 1061, and how does the licensing framework differentiate them? Core

▾
Model Answer
Act 1061 separates insurance business into two broad classes:
  • Life insurance — covering life assurance, annuities, and linked long-term business
  • Non-life (general) insurance — covering fire, motor, marine, accident, liability, and other short-term risks
Composite licences (holding both simultaneously) are generally not permitted for new entrants, preserving a clean separation of funds. Beyond the insurer level, the NIC separately licences:
  • Insurance brokers
  • Insurance agents
  • Loss adjusters
  • Reinsurers and reinsurance brokers
  • Insurance managers
Each category has distinct minimum capital, fit-and-proper, and conduct requirements. Enforcement of licensing conditions — including ensuring no entity operates without a valid licence — is a direct function of the market conduct and enforcement division.
04
SolvencyRisk-Based

Explain the concept of risk-based supervision and why Ghana's NIC adopted it. Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
Risk-Based Supervision (RBS) is a supervisory approach that allocates regulatory resources according to the probability and impact of risks posed by each licensee, rather than treating all entities identically (compliance-based supervision).

Under RBS, the NIC assesses each insurer's risk profile across dimensions including underwriting risk, claims management, governance, investment risk, and market conduct risk. Entities with higher risk profiles receive more intensive supervisory attention — more frequent on-site inspections, more granular data submissions, and tighter capital requirements.

Ghana adopted RBS because:
  • The industry's diversity makes uniform treatment inefficient
  • It aligns with IAIS Insurance Core Principles (especially ICP 9 on supervisory review)
  • It enables the NIC to be proactive — identifying problems before insolvency — rather than reactive
  • It creates incentives for insurers to improve their own risk management
From an enforcement standpoint, RBS shapes how I would prioritise investigations and on-site examinations.
05
IAISInternational

What are the IAIS Insurance Core Principles most relevant to an enforcement and market conduct role? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
The International Association of Insurance Supervisors (IAIS) ICPs most directly relevant to this role include:
  • ICP 18 — Intermediaries: Supervision of brokers and agents, including conduct, competency, and disclosure obligations
  • ICP 19 — Conduct of Business: Requirements for fair treatment of customers, product disclosure, sales practices, and claims handling
  • ICP 10 — Preventive and Corrective Measures: The supervisor's authority to require remediation, impose penalties, and escalate to enforcement action
  • ICP 11 — Enforcement: Powers to sanction, suspend, or revoke licences and to take legal action
  • ICP 25 — Supervisory Cooperation and Coordination: Relevant when dealing with cross-border entities or reinsurers
Understanding how Ghana's Act 1061 maps to these ICPs helps frame enforcement decisions within an internationally defensible framework.
06
Compulsory Insurance

What categories of insurance are compulsory in Ghana, and what enforcement challenges do they present? Core

▾
Model Answer
Ghana mandates several classes of insurance, the most significant being:
  • Third-Party Motor Insurance — required under the Motor Traffic and Transport Act for all vehicles on public roads
  • Employer's Liability / Workmen's Compensation Insurance — required under the Workmen's Compensation Act
  • Professional Indemnity Insurance — required for certain licensed professions (e.g., engineers, architects, lawyers in some contexts)
  • Buildings Insurance — required under the Home Mortgage Finance Act for mortgaged properties
Enforcement challenges include:
  • Widespread use of fake/forged motor insurance stickers — combating this requires coordination with DVLA and Ghana Police Service
  • Low awareness and compliance rates, especially in informal sectors
  • Difficulty verifying coverage without a centralised, real-time insurance database
  • Insurers issuing policies below the mandatory minimum cover in exchange for lower premiums (rate undercutting)
Establishing a robust motor insurance database and strengthening inter-agency collaboration are key enforcement responses.
07
Anti-Money Laundering

How does AML/CFT regulation intersect with insurance supervision in Ghana, and what obligations does it place on insurers? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
Ghana's Anti-Money Laundering Act, 2020 (Act 1044) and the Anti-Terrorism Act designate insurance companies as Reporting Entities subject to AML/CFT obligations overseen by both the NIC and the Financial Intelligence Centre (FIC).

Key insurer obligations include:
  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD) — verifying policyholder identity, beneficial owners, and PEPs at onboarding and throughout the relationship
  • Suspicious Transaction Reporting (STR) — filing reports with the FIC when transactions suggest money laundering or terrorist financing
  • Record-keeping — maintaining transaction and customer records for at least 6 years
  • Internal AML controls — appointing a compliance officer, conducting staff training, and maintaining a risk-based AML programme
From an enforcement perspective, the NIC assesses AML compliance during on-site examinations. Failures can result in administrative sanctions, licence conditions, or referral to the FIC. Life insurers pose a higher AML risk due to high-value single-premium products that can be used to launder funds.
08
Fit & ProperGovernance

What does the "fit and proper" requirement mean in insurance regulation, and how would you assess it during a licensing review? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
The fit and proper requirement applies to directors, key management personnel, and significant shareholders of licensed insurance entities. It ensures those in positions of control or influence are competent and of good character.

Assessment typically covers three dimensions:
  • Probity / Integrity — no history of fraud, dishonesty, or financial crime; no adverse court findings; disclosure of any past regulatory sanctions
  • Competence — relevant educational qualifications, professional credentials (e.g., ACII, FLMI), and adequate experience in insurance or financial services
  • Financial soundness — no record of insolvency, unpaid judgements, or circumstances suggesting the person could not act in the best interests of policyholders
In practice, I would verify declarations against police clearance certificates, credit bureau reports, corporate registry records, and references from previous regulators (especially for cross-border appointees). Ongoing monitoring is equally important — a fit and proper person at licensing may later become subject to adverse findings that require re-assessment.

Market Conduct & Consumer Protection
8 Questions
09
Market ConductDefinition

How would you define "market conduct" in insurance regulation, and why is it distinct from prudential supervision? Core

▾
Model Answer
Market conduct supervision focuses on how insurers and intermediaries behave in their dealings with customers and the marketplace — i.e., whether they treat customers fairly, sell products honestly, and handle claims promptly and justly.

Prudential supervision, by contrast, focuses on whether an insurer is financially sound enough to meet its obligations — monitoring capital adequacy, reserves, and solvency margins.

The distinction matters because a financially healthy insurer can still engage in unfair market conduct (e.g., misleading sales, unjust claim denials), and vice versa. Market conduct failures directly harm individual policyholders, erode public trust in insurance, and reduce penetration rates. In Ghana, where insurance penetration remains below 2% of GDP, poor market conduct is a major barrier to growth — making this supervisory function strategically important, not merely reactive.
10
TCFConsumer

What does "Treating Customers Fairly" (TCF) mean in an insurance context, and how would you operationalise it at the NIC? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
TCF is a principles-based consumer protection framework that holds insurers and intermediaries to fair outcomes across the entire customer lifecycle. The six commonly recognised TCF outcomes are:
  • Customers can be confident they are dealing with firms where fair treatment is central to culture
  • Products and services are designed to meet the needs of identified customer groups
  • Customers are provided with clear information before, during, and after sale
  • Where advice is given, it is suitable and based on customer circumstances
  • Products perform as firms have led customers to expect
  • Claims and complaints are handled fairly, promptly, and without unreasonable barriers
To operationalise TCF at the NIC, I would advocate for: issuing a formal TCF framework/guideline; incorporating TCF metrics into on-site examination templates; requiring insurers to self-assess and report on TCF outcomes; and using mystery shopping and complaints data analysis to identify systemic market conduct failures.
11
ClaimsEnforcement

An insurer is systematically denying or delaying legitimate claims. Walk us through how you would investigate and respond to this. Advanced

▾
Model Answer
I would approach this in structured phases:

1. Intelligence gathering: Review complaints data lodged with the NIC against the insurer. Identify patterns — are denials concentrated in a particular product line, branch, or period? Cross-reference with the insurer's own reported claims settlement ratios.

2. Formal notification: Issue a notice of investigation to the insurer under the relevant provisions of Act 1061, specifying the scope and timeline. This triggers record preservation obligations.

3. Off-site review: Request claims registers, denial letters, internal claims guidelines, and governance committee minutes for the relevant period. Analyse the stated grounds for denial against policy terms.

4. On-site examination: Deploy an inspection team to interview claims staff, review individual files, and assess whether denials reflect genuine policy exclusions or are being used to manage financial performance.

5. Findings and response: Issue a draft findings report; allow the insurer a response period. If breaches are confirmed, remedial actions could include: directing payment of wrongly denied claims, imposing an administrative fine, placing licence conditions on claims handling, requiring an independent claims audit, or — in severe or repeated cases — suspending the licence.

Throughout, I would keep affected policyholders informed where appropriate and consider whether the matter warrants a public notice to the market.
12
Mis-sellingIntermediaries

What regulatory tools does the NIC have to address mis-selling by insurance brokers and agents? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
Mis-selling — selling a product that does not match customer needs, using false representations, or omitting material information — is a core market conduct breach. The NIC's tools include:
  • Licensing conditions — imposing specific sales conduct requirements as a condition of maintaining a broker/agent licence
  • Administrative fines — sanctioning the intermediary financially per Act 1061
  • Licence suspension or revocation — for serious or repeated mis-selling; revoked agents/brokers are placed on a public debarment register
  • Directing redress — requiring the insurer whose product was mis-sold to make affected customers whole (e.g., premium refunds, cancellation without penalty)
  • Market-wide guidance — issuing circulars or conduct guidelines to the industry when a mis-selling pattern is systemic
  • Criminal referral — where mis-selling involves deliberate fraud or misrepresentation, referral to the Attorney-General
Preventively, I would push for mandatory product training requirements for agents, standardised key facts documents, and cooling-off periods for life insurance products.
13
ComplaintsConsumer

How should a robust complaints-handling framework work at both the insurer and regulatory level? Core

▾
Model Answer
At the insurer level, a robust complaints framework should include:
  • A dedicated, easily accessible complaints channel (phone, email, branch, digital)
  • Acknowledgement within 48 hours and final resolution within a defined period (e.g., 30 days)
  • An escalation path to a senior complaints officer or ombudsman function
  • Root cause analysis — using complaint data to fix underlying product or process failures
  • Quarterly reporting of complaints data to the NIC, disaggregated by type, product, and resolution outcome
At the NIC level:
  • The Commission should operate as a complaints registry and escalation body — receiving complaints the insurer has failed to resolve satisfactorily
  • Use complaint trend data as a supervisory intelligence tool — spikes in complaints about a particular insurer or product type should trigger enhanced scrutiny
  • Consider establishing or empowering a formal Insurance Ombudsman for independent adjudication of disputes below a threshold value
14
PremiumRate Undercutting

Premium undercutting and rate competition have historically destabilised Ghana's insurance market. How should the NIC respond? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
Excessive rate undercutting leads to inadequate reserves, inability to pay claims, and ultimately insolvency — damaging policyholders and the entire industry's reputation.

The NIC's response toolkit includes:
  • Minimum premium rates: For motor and some other classes, the NIC can prescribe tariff floors. Act 1061 supports this through rate-filing and approval requirements for certain products.
  • Actuarial pricing reviews: Requiring insurers to have rates certified by a qualified actuary and demonstrate that premiums are adequate to cover expected claims and expenses.
  • Increased capital/reserving charges: Where an insurer is writing at unsustainable prices, requiring higher technical reserves is an indirect corrective mechanism.
  • Market conduct examination: Examining whether low premiums are being achieved by systematically denying claims — in which case the conduct breach is the enforcement target.
  • Industry dialogue: Working with the Ghana Insurers Association (GIA) to build market discipline and professionalism norms.
The balance is to prevent destructive competition without eliminating legitimate price competition that benefits consumers.
15
Digital / Insurtech

How does the rise of digital insurance and insurtech platforms create new market conduct risks, and how should the NIC regulate them? Advanced

▾
Model Answer
Digital channels and insurtechs introduce both opportunities and novel market conduct risks:

Key risks:
  • Algorithmic mis-pricing or bias — models that discriminate unfairly against certain demographics
  • Inadequate digital disclosure — policy terms buried in dense digital text consumers don't read
  • Unlicensed distribution via mobile apps — technology platforms acting as de facto insurance intermediaries without a broker licence
  • Data privacy breaches — excessive collection or misuse of customer data, including health data for life insurance
  • Claims friction in automated systems — algorithms that reject claims without adequate human review or appeal mechanisms
Regulatory responses:
  • Establish a regulatory sandbox for insurtechs — enabling supervised innovation before full licencing
  • Require all entities distributing insurance digitally to hold or operate under an appropriate NIC licence
  • Issue digital conduct guidelines addressing disclosure standards, algorithmic fairness, and data handling
  • Coordinate with the Data Protection Commission on customer data governance
The NIC should be a forward-looking, technology-aware regulator rather than one that is always catching up.
16
Insurance Fraud

What types of insurance fraud are most prevalent in Ghana, and how would you approach enforcement against them? Advanced

▾
Model Answer
Insurance fraud in Ghana manifests across both policyholders and industry participants:

Common fraud types:
  • Motor claims fraud — staged accidents, inflated repair costs, claims for pre-existing damage
  • Fake insurance stickers — forged third-party motor certificates circulating in the market
  • Premium diversion — agents and brokers collecting premiums but failing to remit to insurers, leaving policyholders unknowingly uninsured
  • Life insurance fraud — falsified death certificates, fictitious policies, or insured persons concealing pre-existing conditions
  • Ghost policies — insurance intermediaries issuing fictitious policies to collect commissions
Enforcement approach:
  • Establish or support a dedicated Insurance Fraud Unit in coordination with the Ghana Police Service and Economic and Organised Crime Office (EOCO)
  • Require insurers to maintain and share a fraud register via the GIA or NIC — blacklisting known fraudulent claimants and intermediaries
  • Mandate robust Know Your Customer (KYC) checks at policy inception
  • Advocate for a centralised motor insurance database integrated with DVLA to eliminate fake stickers
  • Refer criminal fraud cases to the Attorney-General — administrative action alone is insufficient for deliberate fraud

Enforcement Practice & Investigations
7 Questions
17
InspectionOn-site

What is the difference between a routine on-site examination and a special/targeted inspection, and when would you recommend each? Core

▾
Model Answer
Routine on-site examinations are scheduled, periodic, and comprehensive. They assess an insurer's overall compliance across governance, financials, claims, market conduct, and AML. They are planned in advance, and the licensee is typically given prior notice. The frequency is determined by the entity's risk rating under the RBS framework — higher-risk entities are examined more frequently.

Special/targeted inspections are triggered by specific intelligence: a spike in complaints, whistleblower information, unusual financial data, or media reports suggesting misconduct. They are focused on a particular issue or business area. Notice may be shorter or, in serious cases, without prior notice to prevent document destruction.

I would recommend a special inspection when: there is credible evidence of imminent harm to policyholders; financial indicators suggest insolvency risk; or a pattern of complaints suggests a systemic breach that requires real-time data to assess.

Both feed into the NIC's supervisory risk register and inform the overall enforcement response.
18
ProportionalitySanctions

How do you ensure that enforcement action is proportionate, consistent, and legally defensible? Advanced

▾
Model Answer
Proportionality is central to credible enforcement. An enforcement action that is excessive risks judicial challenge; one that is too lenient fails to deter future breaches or protect policyholders.

I would ensure proportionality through:
  • A written enforcement policy — publicly available, setting out how the NIC assesses the severity of breaches and the range of responses available
  • Graduated response ladder — from informal guidance, to formal warnings, to licence conditions, to fines, to suspension, to revocation; escalating through the ladder only as warranted
  • Aggravating and mitigating factors — considering the seriousness of harm, whether it was deliberate, whether the licensee cooperated, and whether remediation has been attempted
  • Due process — ensuring the licensee is given findings in writing, a fair opportunity to respond, and clear reasons for any adverse decision
  • Internal peer review — significant enforcement decisions should be reviewed by a panel, not made by a single officer
  • Documentation — maintaining a complete evidential record that could withstand judicial review or appeal to a tribunal
Consistency across similar cases is equally important for regulatory credibility and market confidence.
19
WhistleblowingIntelligence

A whistleblower contacts the NIC alleging that a senior executive at a major insurer is diverting policyholders' premium funds for personal use. How do you handle this? Advanced

▾
Model Answer
This is a serious allegation combining a financial crime (misappropriation) with a conduct breach. My handling would be:

1. Protect the whistleblower — ensure confidentiality; do not disclose the source to the subject institution. Ghana's whistleblower protections under the Whistleblower Act, 2006 (Act 720) apply.

2. Triage the allegation — assess credibility and specificity. Is the whistleblower an insider with direct knowledge? Is the allegation supported by any documentary evidence?

3. Off-site intelligence gathering — without alerting the institution, request and analyse recent financial submissions: premium income reports, escrow/trust account statements, and reinsurance records.

4. Coordinate internally — escalate to senior NIC management and legal counsel. Given the potential criminal dimension, consider early coordination with EOCO or the Ghana Police CID.

5. Unannounced inspection — if financial analysis supports the allegation, conduct an immediate unannounced inspection, securing access to accounts, bank records, and the executive's correspondence.

6. Interim protective measures — if policyholders are at risk, consider immediately directing the insurer to freeze relevant accounts, requiring board-level oversight, or appointing a temporary controller.

7. Refer criminal matter — if evidence of fraud is confirmed, refer to the Attorney-General for prosecution. NIC action (licence suspension, removal of the executive) runs in parallel.
20
InsolvencyRun-off

An insurer's solvency position has deteriorated below the regulatory minimum. What steps should the NIC take, and in what sequence? Advanced

▾
Model Answer
The NIC's response to an insolvency-risk situation involves graduated escalation to protect policyholders while preserving resolution options:

Early intervention (solvency warning zone):
  • Issue a formal Early Warning Letter requiring the insurer to explain the deterioration and submit a corrective action plan within a defined period
  • Enhance supervisory monitoring — increase reporting frequency, request board minutes and actuarial reviews
Remedial action (significant breach):
  • Direct the insurer to cease writing new business in certain lines pending recapitalisation
  • Require an independent actuarial or financial review
  • Restrict dividend payments and related-party transactions
  • Require the board to present and implement a capital restoration plan
Resolution (failure imminent):
  • Appoint a conservator or administrator under Act 1061 to take control of the insurer's affairs and protect existing policyholders
  • Explore portfolio transfer — transferring the book of policies to a solvent insurer
  • As a last resort, initiate orderly wind-down under judicial supervision, ensuring policy obligations are prioritised in the liquidation waterfall
Throughout, proactive policyholder communication is essential to prevent a panic-driven run that accelerates insolvency.
21
Inter-agencyCoordination

The NIC does not operate in isolation. How would you manage coordination with the Bank of Ghana, SEC, FIC, and EOCO in enforcement matters? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
Financial sector enforcement in Ghana increasingly requires cross-agency coordination:
  • Bank of Ghana (BoG) — relevant for bancassurance arrangements, where bank branches distribute insurance products. A mis-selling issue may require joint action. Also relevant for entities licensed by both regulators (e.g., holding companies).
  • Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) — relevant for unit-linked life insurance or investment-linked products that may involve securities. The boundary between insurance and investment products requires coordination.
  • Financial Intelligence Centre (FIC) — for AML/CFT matters: the NIC supervises insurers' AML compliance; the FIC receives STRs and has investigative authority. Joint protocols for intelligence sharing are essential.
  • EOCO / Ghana Police Service — for criminal enforcement referrals: insurance fraud, premium diversion, and fraudulent schemes. The NIC provides regulatory expertise; law enforcement provides coercive powers.
In practice, I would advocate for formalised Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs) with each agency, regular inter-agency working groups, and clear protocols for which agency leads on what category of case. Fragmented enforcement wastes resources and allows regulatory arbitrage.
22
Regulatory Challenge

A licensee is challenging the NIC's enforcement action in court. How would you ensure the NIC's position is defensible? Advanced

▾
Model Answer
Judicial challenges are a normal part of a mature enforcement regime. To ensure the NIC's position is defensible:
  • Statutory authority — confirm that every enforcement step taken is explicitly authorised by Act 1061 or subsidiary legislation, with the specific provisions cited in the decision notice
  • Evidence quality — all findings must be based on documented, verifiable evidence gathered through proper procedures. Hearsay or improperly obtained material weakens the case significantly
  • Due process compliance — ensure the licensee was given: written notice of the alleged breach, sufficient time to respond, an opportunity to be heard (if required), and written reasons for the decision
  • Proportionality record — document that mitigating factors were considered and that the sanction was commensurate with the breach
  • Consistent precedent — demonstrate that similar cases have been treated similarly; arbitrary inconsistency is a ground for judicial review
  • Early legal counsel — engage the NIC's legal team from the moment an enforcement action is likely to be contested, not only when court papers arrive
A strong enforcement culture is one where staff draft decisions as if they will be read by a judge — because sometimes, they will be.
23
Unlicensed Operators

How would you detect and respond to entities operating insurance business without a valid NIC licence? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
Unlicensed operation is one of the most direct threats to policyholders — they may believe they are covered when they are not.

Detection methods:
  • Monitor social media, radio adverts, and online platforms for insurance solicitations not linked to a known licensee
  • Investigate complaints from consumers who discover their "insurer" cannot be traced
  • Regular checks against the Registrar-General's Department — entities registered as insurance companies but not licenced with the NIC
  • Intelligence from licensed insurers and brokers about market participants they know to be operating informally
  • Cross-check mobile money and fintech platforms offering insurance-like products
Response:
  • Issue a cease and desist order immediately on identification
  • Publish a public warning notice naming the entity, to protect consumers still holding its products
  • Refer the matter to the Attorney-General for prosecution — operating without a licence is a criminal offence under Act 1061
  • Seize assets where policyholders' funds are at risk
Prevention requires maintaining a public register of licensed entities that is current and easily searchable.

Competency & Situational Questions
4 Questions
24
MotivationPersonal Fit

Why are you drawn to a regulatory enforcement role at the NIC specifically, as opposed to working within an insurance company? Core

▾
Model Answer Framework
Tailor this to your own story, but strong answers typically address:
  • Public interest orientation — working on the regulatory side means your mandate is to protect all policyholders and the integrity of the market, not the commercial interest of a single entity
  • Breadth of exposure — an enforcement officer sees the full landscape of the market, investigates multiple entities, and develops a panoramic view that an internal compliance role cannot match
  • Impact at system level — a single enforcement action or policy change can improve outcomes for thousands of policyholders simultaneously
  • Alignment with the NIC's mandate — reference specific aspects of the NIC's strategic plan or enforcement priorities that resonate with your professional values
Avoid answers that are simply about job security or compensation. Panel members want to see genuine regulatory vocation.
25
EthicsIntegrity

A senior official from an insurance company you are examining calls your personal number and offers you a "gift" for a favourable report. What do you do? Core

▾
Model Answer
This is an attempt at corruption — it is unambiguous and requires a clear, immediate response:
  • Decline categorically — make clear to the caller that the offer is declined and that it constitutes an improper attempt to influence a regulatory process
  • Document immediately — record the date, time, nature of the call, what was offered, and who made the offer
  • Report to line management — escalate to my direct supervisor and the NIC's internal integrity or compliance unit without delay
  • Consider formal reporting — depending on NIC protocols, this may need to be reported to the Commission of Human Rights and Administrative Justice (CHRAJ) or referred as a potential criminal act under Ghana's anti-bribery laws
  • Recuse if necessary — consider whether my continued involvement in this particular examination is appropriate given the contact, or whether to hand over to a colleague to preserve the integrity of the process
Regulatory credibility depends entirely on the integrity of its officers. Tolerance of even minor corruption destroys institutional trust and undermines every enforcement action the NIC takes.
26
PrioritisationWorkload

You have four active investigations, two on-site examinations due, and a new urgent complaint from a large policyholder. How do you prioritise? Intermediate

▾
Model Answer
I would apply a risk-and-harm framework to prioritise:
  • Urgency of consumer harm — the new complaint from a large policyholder goes to the top of the triage list: assess within 24 hours whether it involves imminent financial loss or denial of a time-sensitive claim. If yes, it gets immediate attention regardless of the queue.
  • Stage of active investigations — investigations at a critical stage (e.g., a deadline for the licensee to respond, or a court-ordered timeline) cannot slip without consequence; prioritise those first among the four.
  • On-site examination preparation — if examinations are legally scheduled with notice given, rescheduling has reputational and legal implications. Confirm whether deferral is possible; if not, allocate team resources accordingly.
  • Delegation and team management — a strong enforcement officer leverages their team. Assign earlier-stage investigation tasks to junior officers with clear briefs and check-in points.
  • Communicate proactively — if deadlines must shift, notify supervisors and affected parties early, with revised timelines. Regulators who miss their own timelines undermine their credibility with licensees.
27
Industry DevelopmentVision

What is the single biggest challenge facing the Ghanaian insurance market, and how should the NIC's enforcement and market conduct function address it? Advanced

▾
Model Answer Framework
There is no single right answer — the panel wants to see strategic thinking and a genuine perspective. Strong answers might focus on one of:

Option 1 — Low penetration driven by trust deficit: Ghana's insurance penetration is among the lowest in Africa. The root cause is consumer distrust — built up over years of claim denials, mis-selling, and insurer insolvencies. Enforcement has a direct role: every successful enforcement action that results in fair treatment of policyholders rebuilds trust one case at a time. The NIC should publish anonymised enforcement outcomes publicly to signal to the market that conduct matters.

Option 2 — Fake motor insurance: The prevalence of counterfeit third-party motor certificates is both a consumer protection failure and a revenue leakage problem for legitimate insurers. The enforcement response requires a real-time, digitalised motor insurance database accessible to police and DVLA — removing the information gap that makes fakes viable.

Option 3 — Premium adequacy and market stability: Chronic rate undercutting means many insurers are not pricing sustainably. The enforcement and market conduct function can support stability by ensuring actuarial certification requirements are actually enforced and that claims denial patterns (a symptom of underpricing) are aggressively investigated.

Whichever option you choose, link the challenge clearly to enforcement and market conduct levers — this is what makes the answer role-relevant.
💡 Preparation Tip: Beyond these Q&As, familiarise yourself with the NIC's most recent Annual Report, any published enforcement circulars or guidelines on the NIC website, and the Ghana Insurers Association (GIA) market statistics. Knowing current penetration rates, number of licensed entities, and any recent high-profile enforcement actions will demonstrate that your interest in the role is current and grounded.
